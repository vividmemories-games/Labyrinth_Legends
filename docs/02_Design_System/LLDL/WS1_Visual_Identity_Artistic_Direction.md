## Document Authority

**WS1 is the artistic identity authority within the Labyrinth Legends Design Language (LLDL).**

| Conflict type | Authority |
|---------------|-----------|
| Product intent (genre, audience, non-goals) | [Vision](../../00_Project/Vision.md) wins |
| Design philosophy (why the game should look and feel as it does) | [WS0 — Design Philosophy](WS0_Design_Philosophy.md) wins |
| Artistic identity (what the game should look like) | **WS1 wins** |
| Color language, environment language, UI language, motion language (future specs) | Must align with WS1 — WS1 wins on identity conflict |
| Token values and component APIs | [Design_Tokens](../Design_Tokens.md) · [Components](../Components.md) — must align with WS1 and [LLDL](LLDL.md) |

WS1 extends [WS0](WS0_Design_Philosophy.md). It does not override WS0 pillars or [Vision](../../00_Project/Vision.md) product intent. When artistic execution conflicts with WS0 philosophical intent, WS0 prevails until [Decisions](../../00_Project/Decisions.md) records an explicit, Human-approved exception.

WS0 defines *why* the visual language exists. **WS1 defines what that language looks like** across every surface of the product.

## 1. Purpose of the Visual Identity

Labyrinth Legends must be **instantly recognizable** — not as a genre, but as a place.

The visual identity exists to ensure that every environment, object, illustration, effect, and branded surface reads as belonging to the same forgotten civilization: temples where stone architecture and mystical systems were built as one discipline.

WS1 answers the question WS0 does not:

> **What does this world look like when multiple artists work independently across years of production?**

Without artistic authority, teams produce competent assets that fail to cohere. With WS1, independent work still converges on a single universe.

### Scope of Authority

WS1 governs artistic identity for:

- Ruins, chambers, and traversal spaces
- Props, mechanisms, and inscribed surfaces
- Explorer and entity design
- Symbolic and decorative language
- Illustration and key art direction
- Marketing and brand imagery
- Atmospheric and compositional standards

WS1 does not govern implementation, production scheduling, or technical delivery. Those belong in downstream specifications.

### Design Intent

Establish permanent artistic direction so recognition survives staff changes, vendor rotation, and technology shifts.

## 2. Artistic Vision

The artistic vision of Labyrinth Legends is **archaeological reverence meeting restrained supernatural engineering**.

The player does not enter a cartoon dungeon or a sci-fi corridor. They enter **ruins that remember their makers** — spaces where geometry was sacred, energy was inscribed, and every threshold implied intention.

### Vision Statement

> A civilization that built temples as instruments — where carved stone, engraved metal, runic light, and portal depth still speak to those patient enough to read them.

### Recognizable at a Glance

A player or observer should identify Labyrinth Legends through a combination of qualities rarely found together in mobile puzzle titles:

- **Monumental stone architecture** with human-scale wear
- **Inscribed surfaces** rather than printed interfaces
- **Controlled luminous energy** emerging from within the ruin
- **Deep atmospheric darkness** relieved by deliberate light
- **Premium restraint** — detail in service of hierarchy, not spectacle

### Artistic North Star

Every asset should feel as though it was **excavated and understood**, not assembled from a generic fantasy kit.

### Design Intent

Give art leadership a single sentence and a single standard for evaluating whether work belongs in this universe.

## 3. Core Artistic Pillars

Six artistic pillars translate WS0 design philosophy into evaluative art-direction standards. A visual proposal that violates any pillar requires explicit Human approval and a [Decisions](../../00_Project/Decisions.md) entry.

### Pillar 1 — Monumental Ruin

Spaces and objects communicate **scale, age, and permanence**. The civilization is gone; the architecture remains. Nothing appears freshly manufactured.

### Pillar 2 — Inscribed Intelligence

Visual language favors **symbols, grooves, frames, and relief** over flat graphic treatment. Information and power appear **written into** the world.

### Pillar 3 — Restrained Luminescence

Light and energy are **precious and intentional**. Glow marks active systems, thresholds, and discovery — never decorative noise across every surface.

### Pillar 4 — Archaeological Authenticity

Materials show **wear, fracture, dust, and patina**. Perfection reads as false. The ruin has lived longer than the player.

### Pillar 5 — Legible Grandeur

Beauty never sacrifices **clarity of form**. Ornate chambers remain readable. Silhouettes remain distinct. Hierarchy remains perceptible.

### Pillar 6 — Unified Ancestry

World art, character design, symbolic language, and promotional imagery share **one material and architectural ancestry**. No parallel visual dialects.

### Design Intent

Provide art reviewers a finite pillar set — preventing subjective debates from reopening foundational identity.

## 4. Visual Identity Overview

The visual identity of Labyrinth Legends is the artistic expression of **Ancient Tech × Mythical Ruins**.

### Identity Formula

**Ancient civilization language** supplies weight, history, and architectural logic.

**Mystical technology language** supplies energy, activation, and the sense that ruins still function.

Their fusion produces a world that feels **discovered**, not decorated.

### Signature Characteristics

- Temple complexes carved into stone and sunken into depth
- Orthogonal and radial geometry derived from sacred architecture
- Engraved metalwork integrated into stone — not bolted on as afterthought
- Runic and crystalline energy contained within channels, frames, and recesses
- Explorer figures scaled against monumental surroundings
- Atmosphere of dust, depth, and controlled mystery
- Emotional temperature of calm tension — never frantic stimulation

### What Identity Is Not

Identity is not a color swap over generic assets. It is not a filter. It is not a single hero prop surrounded by undifferentiated grey stone. Identity is **structural** — expressed through proportion, material, silhouette, and light behavior across the entire frame.

### Design Intent

Condense identity into characteristics artists can verify in thumbnails and greybox reviews.

## 5. Ancient Civilization Language

The ancient civilization layer grounds Labyrinth Legends in **believable human ambition** — builders who invested centuries into stone, ritual geometry, and enduring craft.

### Civilizational Qualities

- **Permanence** — architecture outlasts its creators
- **Intention** — spaces are designed, not naturally occurring caves with props
- **Hierarchy** — grand chambers, processional paths, and focal sanctuaries
- **Reverence** — ornament marks significance; blankness marks passage
- **Decay with dignity** — collapse reads as time, not neglect by the art team

### Archaeological Tone

Ruins should feel **studied**, not looted. The player is an explorer of intelligence, not a vandal. Surfaces preserve enough structure to infer original purpose. Breakage reveals construction, not random rubble piles.

### Cultural Restraint

Avoid identifiable real-world cultures used as costume. The civilization is **original** — inspired by archaeological gravity and temple logic, not by pastiche of a single historical empire.

Motifs may evoke ancient masonry, ritual passage, and sacred geometry without claiming to be Egypt, Mesoamerica, or any specific culture verbatim.

### Design Intent

Ensure environment art reads as **built by people**, preserving emotional weight and narrative credibility.

---

## 6. Mystical Technology Language

Mystical technology is the civilization's **second language** — energy expressed through inscription, crystal, conduit, and portal rather than through modern machine aesthetics.

### Technology Qualities

- **Inscribed** — power flows through carved channels and framed sigils
- **Geometric** — activation respects architectural logic; energy follows structure
- **Responsive** — systems feel dormant until engaged, not constantly noisy
- **Dimensional** — thresholds suggest depth beyond the visible chamber
- **Non-industrial** — no gears-as-decoration, no sci-fi panels, no holographic HUD aesthetic in world space

### Energy Behavior (Artistic)

Energy reads as **contained luminance** — emanating from within stone recesses, crystal cores, or portal volumes. It does not spray across scenes like arcade particles. It **belongs** to the architecture that hosts it.

### Relationship to Ancient Layer

Mystical technology never replaces stone; it **inhabits** stone. The art question is always: *where does the civilization let power enter the built world?*

### Design Intent

Prevent mystical elements from drifting into generic sci-fi or neon fantasy, breaking the Ancient Tech identity.

## 7. Architectural Identity

Architecture is the **primary carrier** of Labyrinth Legends identity. Props and characters succeed when they inherit architectural logic.

### Architectural Principles

- **Chamber clarity** — spaces read as rooms with purpose, not abstract greyboxes
- **Processional flow** — paths, arches, and thresholds guide attention
- **Orthogonal discipline** — grid-aligned structure supports puzzle readability without feeling modern
- **Vertical depth** — pits, shafts, balconies, and sunken floors communicate scale
- **Framed focal points** — altars, gates, exits, and mechanisms sit within intentional frames
- **Modular repetition with variation** — familiar stone vocabulary, distinct chamber identity

### Temple Typology (Artistic)

Chambers may express distinct roles through architecture alone:

- **Threshold spaces** — compression, anticipation, directional light
- **Study chambers** — open floor clarity for observation and planning
- **Mechanism halls** — inscribed apparatus integrated into walls and floors
- **Sanctuary exits** — ceremonial framing of completion
- **Collapsed intervals** — time made visible through broken span and debris logic

Exact level design belongs elsewhere. WS1 owns the **architectural vocabulary** those levels must draw from.

### Design Intent

Give environment artists shared structural language so chambers feel like one civilization across worlds.

## 8. Environmental Storytelling Philosophy

Labyrinth Legends tells story through **space, material, and state** — not through exposition blocks.

### Storytelling Layers

- **Layout** — how rooms connect implies ritual and function
- **Wear patterns** — foot traffic, water staining, repeated touch on sacred edges
- **Broken vs intact** — what survived reveals what mattered
- **Dormant vs active systems** — energy state communicates history and present threat
- **Absence** — empty pedestals, darkened conduits, sealed passages invite inference

### Restraint Rule

Environmental storytelling **supports** curiosity; it does not replace puzzle communication. A beautiful mural that obscures a hazard fails WS1 and WS0 readability obligations.

### Discovery Tone

Revelation should feel like **insight** — the player notices what was always present, correctly interpreted. Not like a cutscene delivering new information unrelated to the chamber.

### Design Intent

Align world art with the explorer fantasy: intelligence rewarded through observation.

## 9. Shape Language

Shape language derives from **architecture, inscription, and sacred geometry** — not from consumer software defaults or cartoon simplification.

### Primary Forms

- **Rectilinear masses** — blocks, lintels, plinths, stepped platforms
- **Arches and framed openings** — thresholds as readable shapes
- **Radial motifs** — sun, portal, and mechanism hubs with controlled symmetry
- **Chamfered stone edges** — worn corners, not perfect mathematical sharpness
- **Recessed panels** — information and energy sit **in** surfaces, not on stickers

### Secondary Forms

- **Crystalline growth** — faceted, bounded, rare
- **Conduit lines** — channels connecting activation points
- **Sigil geometry** — symbolic marks with consistent stroke weight and angular character

### Prohibited Shape Defaults

- Generic rounded mobile-app card silhouettes in world space
- Blob-like props without structural logic
- Excessive spikey fantasy noise without architectural anchor
- Random asymmetry that breaks grid comprehension in puzzle chambers

### Design Intent

Ensure form vocabulary remains recognizable in iconography, props, and environment modules.

---

## 10. Silhouette Philosophy

Silhouette is the **first read** of any asset. If silhouette fails, no surface detail rescues identity.

### Silhouette Principles

- **Distinct at thumbnail scale** — mechanisms, doors, hazards, and landmarks remain identifiable when small
- **Architectural anchoring** — props silhouette as extensions of masonry vocabulary
- **Explorer readability** — player avatar reads clearly against stone without becoming a cartoon outline
- **Energy as contour** — active systems may alter silhouette through contained glow, not shapeless bloom
- **Negative space discipline** — openings, arches, and pits are part of the silhouette composition

### Hierarchy Through Silhouette

Primary interactables carry **stronger silhouette contrast** than background relief. Decorative carving recedes. Functional forms advance.

### Design Intent

Protect gameplay-adjacent readability and marketing recognition through form-before-texture discipline.

## 11. Scale & Proportion Philosophy

Scale communicates **awe, vulnerability, and intention**. Labyrinth Legends juxtaposes human explorers against monumental ruins.

### Scale Principles

- **Monumental environment, human agency** — chambers impress; player remains comprehensible
- **Mechanism legibility** — interactive apparatus remains proportioned for reading, not realism alone
- **Consistent module rhythm** — stone units, step heights, and frame widths feel systematic
- **Exaggeration in service of clarity** — slight scale emphasis on critical elements is permitted when readability demands it
- **No arbitrary giantism** — scale drama must feel architecturally motivated

### Vertical Proportion

Height emphasizes **depth and reverence**. Low ceilings create intimacy and compression. Vaulted or sunken volumes create revelation. Scale shifts are emotional tools, not random variety.

### Design Intent

Prevent assets that float in scale ambiguity — a common failure mode in modular environment production.

---

## 12. Material Philosophy

Materials communicate **history, function, and value** without requiring explanation.

### Primary Materials

- **Stone** — structure, floor, wall, weight; varied geology but unified craft
- **Engraved metal** — authority, binding, reward framing; warm tonal family
- **Crystal** — rare focus, relic significance, contained energy
- **Mineral deposits** — age, moisture, optional biological patina within fantasy restraint

### Material Relationships

Stone is **matte and absorptive**. Metal is **reflective within warm restraint**. Crystal is **transmissive and focal**. Energy is **emissive within channels** — never a material replacement for all three.

### Wear Logic

Wear follows **use and time**:

- Paths polish or erode
- Edges chip where impact is plausible
- Metal tarnishes in recesses; highlights remain on raised relief
- Dust accumulates on horizontal planes and in light beams

### Design Intent

Give surfacing artists shared rules so materials agree across biomes and vendors.

---

## 13. Surface Detail Philosophy

Surface detail expresses **craft and time** at the scale the player perceives.

### Detail Distribution

- **Hero surfaces** — focal mechanisms, exits, relic niches carry richer relief
- **Traversal surfaces** — readable, not noisy; detail supports footing clarity
- **Background mass** — simplified but never flat plastic; retains stone character
- **Near-field vs far-field** — detail density decreases with distance and importance

### Detail Types

- Carved relief and linear inscription
- Fracture, spalling, and edge damage
- Sediment, dust, and moisture staining
- Metal inlay and channel work
- Subtle crystalline intrusion at activation sites

### Detail Anti-Excess

If removing thirty percent of surface noise **improves** silhouette and readability, the asset is over-detailed. Premium craft is **curated**, not maximal.

### Design Intent

Prevent detail from becoming camouflage — a frequent ruin of otherwise strong environment art.

---

## 14. Ornamentation Principles

Ornament marks **significance**. Where ornament is absent, passage should feel deliberate.

### Ornament Functions

- Frame thresholds and sanctuaries
- Mark mechanisms and sealed systems
- Distinguish optional discovery from mandatory path
- Reward mastery and completion symbolically
- Encode civilization aesthetic without readable modern text

### Ornament Qualities

- **Linear and geometric** — engraving, borders, repeating motifs
- **Subordinate to form** — ornament follows architecture; it does not overpower it
- **Consistent motif family** — angular sigils, radial sun forms, portal rings, lattice channels
- **Earned density** — richest ornament at culmination points, not uniformly everywhere

### Ornament Restraint

Ornament must never:

- Obscure hazard or path communication
- Introduce culturally specific sacred imagery treated carelessly
- Transform every wall into equal visual loudness

### Design Intent

Ensure decoration reinforces hierarchy and ceremony rather than becoming visual wallpaper.

## 15. Readability & Visual Hierarchy

Readability is an **artistic obligation**, not a downstream UX patch.

### Hierarchy Layers

1. **Traversal and path expression** — where movement is possible and committed
2. **Threat and blocking elements** — what endangers or constrains the plan
3. **Interactive systems** — what changes state when engaged
4. **Optional reward and mastery signals** — what enriches without gating
5. **Atmospheric depth** — dust, distant architecture, non-interactive grandeur

Layers 1–3 must survive art passes, lighting passes, and promotional cropping.

### Artistic Readability Tools

- Silhouette separation
- Value hierarchy (light/dark structure)
- Material contrast (matte stone vs emissive channel)
- Spatial framing (arches directing gaze)
- Controlled color temperature shifts (warm metal vs cool energy)

### Accessibility Alignment

Critical distinctions must remain perceptible without relying on a single visual channel. Artistic hierarchy supports [WS0](WS0_Design_Philosophy.md) accessibility philosophy; exact requirements live in [Accessibility](../Accessibility.md).

### Design Intent

Bind art direction to player comprehension — consistent with WS0 readability without defining UI behavior.

## 16. Contrast Philosophy

Contrast organizes attention. Labyrinth Legends uses **measured contrast** — never the harsh binary of arcade UI, never the mud of undifferentiated brown ruins.

### Contrast Types

- **Value contrast** — light against temple darkness; focal beams against shadow mass
- **Material contrast** — stone against metal against crystal against energy
- **Scale contrast** — monumental frame against human-scale explorer
- **State contrast** — dormant vs active systems visibly distinct
- **Emotional contrast** — calm baseline with earned peaks at commitment and resolution

### Contrast Restraint

High contrast is a **resource**. Overuse fatigues the eye and destroys hierarchy. Every chamber needs areas of visual rest.

### Design Intent

Give lighting and surfacing leads a shared theory of contrast application across the product.

---

## 17. Composition Philosophy

Composition directs **attention, emotion, and narrative inference** within the frame.

### Compositional Principles

- **Central clarity for puzzle floors** — play space remains comprehensible
- **Framed destinations** — exits and major mechanisms sit at compositional anchors
- **Leading lines** — processional paths, conduit lines, light beams guide the eye
- **Depth staging** — foreground, play layer, and atmospheric background clearly separated
- **Breathing margin** — premium craft allows negative space; clutter is not richness

### Camera-Agnostic Rule

WS1 defines compositional **intent** independent of camera implementation. Whether orthographic, isometric, or otherwise, the frame must preserve hierarchy and focal clarity.

### Design Intent

Ensure key art, chambers, and promotional crops share compositional discipline.

---

## 18. Texture Philosophy

Texture communicates **material truth** at the viewing distance relevant to play and illustration.

### Texture Principles

- **Scale-appropriate** — macro stone grain on near surfaces; simplified read at distance
- **Directional logic** — chisel, erosion, and sediment follow plausible forces
- **Non-repetitive hero zones** — focal areas break tiling subtly without noise
- **Energy does not replace texture** — glow accents stone; it does not erase material identity
- **Illustrative cohesion** — painted and three-dimensional assets share texture temperament

### Texture Families

- Rough igneous and sedimentary stone variation
- Fine engraved line work on metal and panel edges
- Crystalline faceting with internal depth
- Dust and particulate softness in air volumes

### Design Intent

Prevent texture from becoming either muddy noise or sterile flatness — both break premium identity.

---

## 19. Lighting Philosophy

Lighting is described here at **artistic intent only**. Technical delivery belongs in downstream documents.

### Lighting Identity

- **Temple darkness as canvas** — shadow is presence, not failure
- **Motivated sources** — light originates from portals, crystals, shafts, and emissive channels
- **Directional drama** — beams and rims reveal form and depth
- **Restrained fill** — enough to read; not enough to flatten mystery
- **State-readable illumination** — activation visibly alters local light behavior

### Emotional Lighting

- **Arrival** — cool depth, distant warmth
- **Study** — even enough for comprehension; atmosphere preserved
- **Commitment** — focal warming or intensification at decision points
- **Consequence** — directional emphasis on outcome locus
- **Resolution** — ceremonial lift without arcade fireworks

### Design Intent

Align all future lighting direction with identity-bearing motivation — not generic flat mobile brightness.

---

## 20. Atmospheric Identity

Atmosphere is the **continuous emotional medium** of the ruin — dust, depth, air, and subtle motion in light.

### Atmospheric Qualities

- **Dust in light** — time made visible
- **Depth haze** — chambers feel larger than the play floor
- **Soft particulate restraint** — atmosphere supports; it does not obscure
- **Acoustic-visual sympathy** — stillness reads through visual calm (audio spec elsewhere)
- **Biological minimalism** — moss, roots, or water may appear; jungle carnival does not

### Atmospheric Progression

Early spaces teach atmosphere as **safe mystery**. Later spaces intensify depth and contrast without abandoning readability.

### Design Intent

Define the environmental "air" players breathe — a major recognition factor across worlds.

## 21. Emotional Mood Progression

Visual mood tracks the player journey defined in [Vision](../../00_Project/Vision.md) and [WS0](WS0_Design_Philosophy.md).

### Mood Phases

**Arrival** — curiosity, slight unease; wide shadow, distant glow, incomplete information.

**Observation** — focus; composition stabilizes, interactables emerge from atmosphere.

**Planning** — calm tension; path and risk language clarifies without alarm.

**Execution** — anticipation; motion and light respond sequentially to consequence.

**Resolution** — earned satisfaction or instructive failure; ceremonial framing without spectacle excess.

**Return** — confidence; familiar motifs recur with mastered clarity.

### Mood Guardrails

Mood never escalates to panic visuals. Triumph never becomes slot-machine explosion. Failure never becomes punitive mockery.

### Design Intent

Give illustrators and environment leads a mood map independent of narrative script.

## 22. Visual Consistency Rules

Consistency is how artistic identity survives production scale.

### Inheritance Model

```text
Vision (product intent)
    ↓
WS0 (design philosophy)
    ↓
WS1 (visual identity & artistic direction)  ← this document
    ↓
Future language docs (color, environment, UI, motion, etc.)
    ↓
LLDL · Tokens · Components · Screens · Brand
```

Lower layers extend upper layers. They do not silently redefine them.

### Consistency Requirements

- Shared architectural module vocabulary across worlds
- Shared sigil and engraving motif family
- Shared material temperament (stone, metal, crystal, energy)
- Shared proportion rhythm and explorer scale
- Shared atmospheric baseline and contrast discipline
- Shared rejection of identity-breaking anti-patterns (see §23)

### Change Control

New motif families, material classes, or silhouette dialects require WS1 alignment review, [LLDL](LLDL.md) check, Human approval, and [Decisions](../../00_Project/Decisions.md) entry if material.

### Design Intent

Operationalize consistency so review is procedural, not personal taste.

## 23. Visual Anti-Patterns

The following anti-patterns are **incompatible** with Labyrinth Legends identity.

### Identity Anti-Patterns

- Generic mobile UI surfaces placed unmodified in world space
- Cyberpunk neon palette dominating scenes
- Cartoon proportions on explorer or creatures
- Flat untextured stone with sticker detail
- Sci-fi industrial panels in mystical chambers
- Random skull-and-spike fantasy clutter
- Stock "dungeon grey" without material or light identity

### Readability Anti-Patterns

- Ornament competing with path and hazard communication
- Full-scene bloom removing value hierarchy
- Every interactable screaming equal visual priority
- Atmospheric fog obscuring puzzle-critical geometry
- Identical silhouette language for safe and lethal elements

### Craft Anti-Patterns

- Trend-chasing visual styles dated to a single release year
- Overuse of lens effects as substitute for composition
- Photoreal grit without architectural intention
- Maximal detail on non-focal surfaces
- Key art that promises a world the game does not deliver

### Design Intent

Give reviewers explicit rejection language — reducing debate and protecting WS0 pillars.

## 24. Design Decision Framework

Use this framework before approving concept art, environment sets, illustration, or branded materials.

### Evaluation Sequence

1. **Vision alignment** — Does this support product intent?
2. **WS0 alignment** — Does this honor design philosophy and pillars?
3. **WS1 pillar compliance** — Does this satisfy all six artistic pillars?
4. **Recognition** — Is this unmistakably Labyrinth Legends at thumbnail scale?
5. **Readability** — Does hierarchy survive the art pass?
6. **Material truth** — Do surfaces and wear feel archaeologically credible?
7. **Restraint** — Is detail, light, and ornament earned?
8. **Consistency** — Does this inherit established motif and architecture language?
9. **Timelessness** — Will this remain defensible years after ship?

### Decision Outcomes

- **Approve** — proceed to downstream spec or production
- **Revise** — adjust to satisfy failed criteria
- **Reject** — do not produce; cite violated pillar or anti-pattern
- **Escalate** — Human approval and Decisions entry for material exceptions

### Design Intent

Make artistic approval repeatable across directors, vendors, and review agents.

## 25. Summary

Labyrinth Legends looks like **forgotten temples where inscribed architecture and mystical systems still answer to careful thought**.

**WS1 establishes that:**

- Visual identity is Ancient Tech × Mythical Ruins expressed through architecture, material, and restrained light
- Six artistic pillars govern all visual evaluation
- Ancient civilization language supplies weight; mystical technology language supplies responsive energy
- Silhouette, scale, texture, and composition serve readability and emotional pacing
- Atmosphere and mood progression support calm tension across the player journey
- Consistency inheritance flows Vision → WS0 → WS1 → downstream language documents
- Explicit anti-patterns protect identity from generic mobile, sci-fi, and cartoon drift

Every artist working on this project shares one obligation: **build a ruin that feels real, legible, and unmistakably ours.**

Downstream specification of color roles, environment modules, and interface language begins after WS1 approval — starting with [LLDL](LLDL.md) and future LLDL workshop documents.

| 8 | Lighting and atmosphere serve motivated mystery — not flat brightness |
| 9 | Mood progression tracks arrival through return without arcade stimulation |
| 10 | Future color, environment, UI, and motion language docs must inherit WS1 without parallel dialects |

### Design Intent

Freeze workshop outcomes so downstream artistic specifications do not reopen identity fundamentals.
